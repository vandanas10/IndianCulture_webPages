Greetings.

India is known for its diverse cultural traditions, which is reflected in the range of ways people greet each other. Some greetings are more formal, while others are more casual, and often involve gestures like handshakes or hugs.

Namaste

Namaste, also commonly known as Namaskar, is a traditional Hindu greeting that expresses respect, honor, and hospitality. In jainism the same posture is used to greet and they utter "Jai Jinendra". Namaste is a combination of two Sanskrit words: "namah" meaning "bow," and "te" meaning "to you.".' It involves placing both hands together in front of the chest and bowing the head. It is also a common greeting in India, Nepal, and other South Asian countries.

Sat Sri Akaal

Sat Sri Akaal is a common greeting in the Sikh community. The phrase, which means "Truth is the ultimate reality" is often accompanied by the gesture of folding hands in a prayer-like manner and bowing slightly. It is a sign of respect and humility towards others. The greeting is used to convey a sense of unity and brotherhood among the Sikh community and is also used as a farewell.

Vanakkam

Vanakkam is a traditional Tamil greeting commonly used in Tamil Nadu and parts of Sri Lanka. It is a gesture of respect and welcome, with the word itself meaning "greetings" or "salutations". The greeting is often accompanied by the gesture of joining one's palms together and bowing slightly. It is a polite and friendly way to acknowledge others and express one's hospitality.

Adab

Adab is a gesture of respect commonly used in South Asia, especially among Muslim communities. It involves placing the right hand over the heart while slightly bowing the head. Adab is often used as a greeting or farewell and to show respect to elders or individuals of higher authority. The gesture is also used to show gratitude or to express apologies.

As-salamu alaykum

"As-salamu alaykum" is a common Arabic greeting used among Muslims. It means "peace be upon you" and is typically accompanied by a handshake. The right hand is extended while the left hand is placed on the chest as a sign of sincerity. It is also customary to respond with the same greeting, "wa alaykum as-salam," meaning "and upon you be peace."
